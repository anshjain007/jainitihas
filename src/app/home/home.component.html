History of Jainism

Expanded History of Jainism in India:
Jainism is one of the most ancient religions originating in India, with roots tracing back to the Indus Valley Civilization, where evidence of non-violent practices and spiritual symbolism have been discovered. The religion was formally structured by a line of 24 spiritual teachers known as Tirthankaras, the first being Rishabhanatha, who is believed to have lived many millennia ago. Jainism gained prominence during the time of the 24th Tirthankara, Lord Mahavira, who was born in 599 BCE in present-day Bihar. He renounced worldly life at the age of 30 and attained Kevala Jnana (omniscience) after 12 years of intense penance. His teachings emphasized Ahimsa (non-violence), Satya (truth), Asteya (non-stealing), Brahmacharya (chastity), and Aparigraha (non-possessiveness). Jainism flourished under royal patronage during the Maurya dynasty, especially under Chandragupta Maurya, who is said to have embraced Jainism in his later years. The religion saw further growth under southern dynasties like the Chalukyas and Rashtrakutas, who constructed magnificent Jain temples such as those at Shravanabelagola, Ellora, and Mount Abu. Jains made remarkable contributions to Indian philosophy, ethics, art, and literature, promoting a life of compassion, logic, and introspection. Despite being a minority, Jain communities have remained influential, preserving their identity through strict vegetarianism, ritual purity, and active philanthropy. Jainism continues to thrive in modern India, with spiritual centers, monastic orders, and global followers upholding its timeless message of peace and self-realization.
